After years working with enterprise data teams across industries, I keep coming back to the same question: why do most data projects fail to generate operational value?

The tooling isn't the bottleneck. Most enterprises have invested heavily in warehouses, orchestration, and visualization. The problem is that data and decision-making are decoupled. Analysts produce outputs; operators ignore them.

## The integration problem

Palantir's core insight — and what makes Foundry different — is that **the product is the workflow, not the dashboard**.

Instead of building yet another analytics layer, Foundry embeds data into the operational systems where decisions actually get made. Your maintenance schedule isn't just visible in a report — it's directly integrated with the system your field team uses to plan their day.

This sounds simple. It isn't. Most data teams aren't set up to build operational workflows. They're set up to answer questions. That capability gap is where 10x does its best work.

## What this means for leaders

If you're a data leader evaluating Palantir, the question isn't "what can this tool do?" It's "what workflows can we actually change?"

That requires thinking differently about your team structure, your relationship with operations, and your definition of "done." A dashboard shipped is not a problem solved.

## The AIP inflection point

With Artificial Intelligence Platform (AIP), Palantir has gone further — creating a controlled environment where LLMs can act on operational data directly. The implications for enterprise AI adoption are significant: instead of building bespoke AI pipelines, teams can connect AI reasoning to existing Foundry workflows with governance built in from day one.

We're still early. But the trajectory is clear.
